Understanding EEG Signals

EEG is a non-invasive physiological method used to record the electrical activity generated by the brain by detecting synchronous firing of populations of neurons.

Mu Rhythms (8-13 Hz)

Directly associated with motor cortex activity; suppressed during movement execution or imagery.

Beta Rhythms (13-30 Hz)

Informs us about cognitive states and movement preparation.

ii ¬∑ Preprocessing Pipeline

Signal Processing Steps

Raw EEG signals are noisy. Our three-stage pipeline isolates the motor imagery patterns we need.

1

Noise Filtering

Apply a band-pass filter (8-30 Hz) to isolate Mu and Beta frequency bands, removing irrelevant low-frequency drift and high-frequency muscle artifacts.

Output

Clean signal in the 8-30 Hz range

2

Signal Segmentation

Slice continuous recordings into 4-second epochs aligned to motor imagery events. Each epoch becomes one training sample for our model.

Output

Labeled epochs per trial event

3

Normalization

Apply StandardScaler to zero-mean and unit-variance normalize each channel, ensuring consistent amplitude scales across subjects.

Output

ML-ready feature matrix

Wheelchair Control Interface

The trained EEGNet model performs three-class classification: T0 (Rest), T1, and T2. Once a prediction exceeds a predefined confidence threshold, the system encodes the output and transmits it to a microcontroller, which translates the signal into real-time motor commands.

Class T0

Rest State

Represents absence of motor imagery. The system interprets this state as a safety halt command, immediately stopping wheelchair movement.

Class T1

Motor Imagery A

Corresponds to task-dependent motor activation (e.g., left fist or both fists depending on run). This output is mapped to a directional command such as Turn Left or Move Forward.

Class T2

Motor Imagery B

Represents the complementary motor task (e.g., right fist or both feet depending on run). The controller converts this into an alternative navigation command such as Turn Right or controlled stop.
